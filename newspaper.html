Featured Destination

Kyoto, Japan: Where Ancient Tradition
Meets Eternal Beauty

By Eleanor Ashworth — Far East Correspondent • Filed from Higashiyama, Kyoto

There is a stillness in Kyoto that one does not find in the hurried corridors of modern Japan. It lives in the mossy stones of Ryoan-ji, in the vermillion corridors of Fushimi Inari, and in the quiet rustle of bamboo groves that have stood sentinel over this city for more than a thousand years. To visit Kyoto is not merely to travel — it is to step through a threshold into a world where time moves with the deliberate grace of a tea ceremony.

The city's seventeen UNESCO World Heritage Sites are not museum relics but living, breathing spaces. Monks still chant at dawn in Kinkaku-ji's gardens. Geiko still glide through the lantern-lit streets of Gion at dusk. The Philosopher's Path, lined with cherry trees that erupt in clouds of pale pink each April, remains the contemplative walk it has been since Nishida Kitaro paced it a century ago, turning over the great questions of existence.

What strikes the visitor most profoundly is Kyoto's refusal to choose between preservation and vitality. This is a city where a 400-year-old tea house sits comfortably beside a Michelin-starred restaurant reimagining kaiseki cuisine. Where a master calligrapher practices his art in a studio above a vinyl record shop. Kyoto does not fossilize its traditions; it inhabits them.

The traveller would do well to arrive in autumn, when the maples of Tofuku-ji blaze in shades of crimson and gold, and the light falls through the temple eaves with a quality that has inspired poets and painters for generations beyond counting. Reserve at least five days. Kyoto does not yield its secrets to the impatient.

Dispatches From Abroad

Our correspondents report from eight destinations that demand your attention this year

Santorini, Greece

By Marcus Thorne

The whitewashed cliffs of Santorini have graced a thousand postcards, yet nothing prepares the first-time visitor for the sheer drama of this volcanic caldera. The villages of Oia and Fira cling to the rim like barnacles on a ship's hull, their blue-domed churches catching the Aegean light. Arrive in late September, when the summer hordes have thinned and the sunsets over the caldera achieve their most operatic hues. The local Assyrtiko wine, born of volcanic soil, pairs impeccably with fresh-caught octopus at a cliffside taverna.

Patagonia, Argentina & Chile

By Catalina Reyes

At the bottom of the world, where the Andes finally exhaust themselves into the sea, Patagonia sprawls in magnificent desolation. Torres del Paine's granite spires pierce skies of impossible blue. The Perito Moreno glacier groans and calves with primordial force. This is landscape on a scale that humbles — vast grasslands where guanacos roam, forests of ancient lenga beech, and winds that remind the traveller of nature's indifference. The W Trek remains South America's finest multi-day hike. Come prepared for four seasons in a single afternoon.

Marrakech, Morocco

By Idris Belhaj

Marrakech assaults the senses in the most glorious fashion. The medina's labyrinthine souks overflow with pyramids of saffron and cumin, towers of handwoven carpets, and the relentless theatre of Jemaa el-Fnaa square at nightfall. Yet retreat through any carved cedar doorway and you may find yourself in the serene courtyard of a riad, where orange trees shade a marble fountain and the chaos of the city dissolves entirely. This duality — the frenetic and the tranquil, the ancient and the artful — is the city's enduring magic. The Jardin Majorelle alone justifies the journey.

Reykjavik, Iceland

By Sigrid Halldorsdottir

Iceland's compact capital serves as the gateway to a landscape that feels extraterrestrial. Within two hours of Reykjavik, one can stand between tectonic plates at Thingvellir, watch geysers erupt at Geysir, and feel the thunder of Gullfoss cascade into a glacial canyon. The city itself, colorful and fiercely literary, punches well above its weight with world-class restaurants, geothermal pools, and a cultural calendar that defies its population of 140,000. Visit in winter for the Northern Lights; in summer for the midnight sun. Either way, the Blue Lagoon awaits.

The Amalfi Coast, Italy

By Giulia Ferrante

Strung along the vertiginous cliffs south of Naples like jewels on a necklace, the Amalfi Coast remains Italy's most intoxicating stretch of coastline. Positano tumbles down to the sea in a cascade of pastel houses and bougainvillea. Ravello perches above the clouds, its Villa Rufolo gardens inspiring Wagner to compose. The road itself — the SS163 — is a white-knuckle marvel of engineering that delivers a new postcard vista at every hairpin turn. Eat the lemons. Drink the limoncello. Take the boat to Capri. Let the dolce far niente take hold and do not resist it.

Prague, Czech Republic

By Vera Novotná

Prague reveals itself in layers, like the pages of an illuminated manuscript. The Charles Bridge, lined with Baroque statuary, stretches across the Vltava beneath a sky that seems painted by the Old Masters themselves. In the Old Town Square, the Astronomical Clock — six centuries old and still ticking — performs its mechanical pageant on the hour, drawing crowds who crane their necks like medieval pilgrims. Yet it is in the lesser-known quarters that the city truly enchants: the winding lanes of Malá Strana, the jazz cellars of Žižkov, the beer halls where Bohemian lagers have flowed since before Columbus set sail. Prague is a fairy tale that never condescends to explain its own magic.

Machu Picchu, Peru

By Rodrigo Espinoza

The lost citadel of the Inca does not so much appear as materialise. After days on the trail — or a serpentine bus ride from Aguas Calientes — the fog parts to reveal terraces of fitted stone cascading down a ridge so narrow it seems to defy the laws of engineering and gravity alike. At 2,430 metres above sea level, Machu Picchu sits among the clouds with the quiet authority of a place that has outlasted the empire that built it. The Temple of the Sun catches the solstice light as precisely today as it did five centuries ago. One does not visit Machu Picchu; one makes a pilgrimage. Arrive at dawn, when the mist lifts and the silence holds the memory of an entire civilisation.

A Note From the Editors

By The Editorial Board

We have endeavoured in this edition to present not merely a list of destinations but a portfolio of experiences. Each city and landscape featured in these pages was selected not for novelty alone but for a quality we might call resonance — the capacity of a place to linger in the memory long after the passport stamps have faded. We encourage our readers to travel slowly, to wander without agenda, and to remember that the finest souvenirs are not purchased in shops but collected in the quiet moments between departures. Safe travels, dear readers, and do write to us from abroad.
